Use this calculator to estimate your monthly loan payment and total amount paid over the life of an amortizing loan. Useful for mortgage planning, business loans, equipment financing, and any fixed-term borrowing decision.

## What this calculator does

The amortization calculator helps you understand how a loan repays over time by calculating your monthly payment and the total cost of borrowing based on your loan amount, interest rate, and term.

It uses:

- loan principal
- annual interest rate
- loan term in years

This gives you your monthly payment and total amount paid — the two most important numbers for evaluating any loan.

## How to use the amortization calculator

1. Enter your **loan amount** — the total amount you are borrowing
2. Enter your **annual interest rate** — the yearly interest rate on the loan, expressed as a percentage
3. Enter your **loan term** — the number of years over which the loan will be repaid
4. The calculator instantly shows your estimated monthly payment and total amount paid over the full term

The difference between the total amount paid and the original loan amount is the total interest cost — the true price of borrowing.

## Amortization Formula

**Monthly Payment = P × (r(1+r)^n) ÷ ((1+r)^n − 1)**

Where:

- **P** = loan principal
- **r** = monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12)
- **n** = total number of monthly payments (years × 12)

## Example calculation

If:

- Loan amount = **100,000**
- Annual interest rate = **6%**
- Term = **20 years**

Then:

- Monthly rate = **0.5%**
- Number of payments = **240**
- Monthly payment = **716.43**
- Total paid = **171,943**
- Total interest = **71,943**

Over 20 years, borrowing 100,000 at 6% costs an additional 71,943 in interest — more than 70% of the original loan amount.

## What is amortization?

Amortization is the process of paying off a loan through regular fixed payments over a set period. Each payment covers both interest and a portion of the principal balance.

In the early stages of an amortizing loan, most of each payment goes toward interest. As the loan progresses, a larger share of each payment reduces the principal. By the final payment, almost all of it is principal repayment.

This structure is used for mortgages, car loans, business loans, and most other fixed-term lending products.

## How amortization works over the loan term

Understanding how your payments are split between interest and principal helps you make better borrowing decisions:

- **Early payments** — heavily weighted toward interest, with very little principal reduction
- **Mid-term payments** — a more even split between interest and principal
- **Late payments** — mostly principal repayment, with very little interest remaining

This is why paying extra toward the principal early in a loan term reduces total interest paid significantly — every extra payment directly reduces the balance on which future interest is calculated.

## What is a good monthly payment for a business loan?

There is no universal answer — it depends on your revenue, cash flow, and the purpose of the loan. A common guideline is that total debt service should not exceed 10% to 15% of monthly revenue for most small businesses. ## Why amortization matters for loan planning

Understanding amortization helps you:

- calculate the true monthly cost of any loan before committing
- compare the total cost of different loan terms side by side
- understand how much of each payment goes to interest vs principal
- evaluate whether a shorter term with higher payments saves money overall
- avoid surprises when financing business equipment, property, or working capital

## How loan term affects total cost

Longer loan terms mean lower monthly payments but significantly higher total interest paid:

- A 100,000 loan at 6% over **10 years** — monthly payment ~1,110, total interest ~33,000
- A 100,000 loan at 6% over **20 years** — monthly payment ~716, total interest ~72,000
- A 100,000 loan at 6% over **30 years** — monthly payment ~600, total interest ~116,000

Choosing a longer term to reduce monthly payments dramatically increases the total cost of borrowing. Always calculate the full picture before deciding on a term.

## Common mistakes when calculating loan amortization

Common mistakes include:

- focusing only on the monthly payment without calculating total interest paid
- ignoring fees, arrangement costs, and insurance that add to the true cost of borrowing
- forgetting that variable rate loans can change the payment structure over time
- assuming that a lower monthly payment always means a better deal

## FAQs

### What is amortization?

Amortization is the process of repaying a loan over time through regular fixed payments. Each payment covers both interest and a portion of the principal until the loan is fully paid off.

### How do you calculate monthly loan payments?

Monthly Payment = P × (r(1+r)^n) ÷ ((1+r)^n − 1), where P is the principal, r is the monthly interest rate, and n is the total number of payments.

### What is the difference between amortization and a simple interest loan?

An amortizing loan has a fixed payment structure where interest and principal are paid together over time. A simple interest loan calculates interest only on the outstanding principal without the structured repayment schedule.

### Why does the total amount paid exceed the loan amount?

The difference is the total interest charged over the loan term. The longer the term and the higher the rate, the greater the gap between the original loan amount and the total repaid.

### Is it better to choose a shorter or longer loan term?

A shorter term means higher monthly payments but significantly less total interest paid. A longer term reduces monthly payments but increases the total cost of borrowing considerably. The right choice depends on your cash flow and the purpose of the loan.

### Can I reduce total interest by making extra payments?

Yes. Any extra payment directly reduces the principal balance, which reduces the interest calculated on future payments. Even small additional payments early in the loan term can save a significant amount over the full term.

### What types of loans use amortization?

Most fixed-term loans use amortization — including mortgages, business term loans, car loans, and equipment financing. Revolving credit facilities like business credit cards or lines of credit do not use fixed amortization schedules.

### How does interest rate affect monthly payments?

A higher interest rate increases both the monthly payment and the total interest paid over the life of the loan. Even a 1% difference in rate on a large loan can mean thousands in additional cost over a long term.
