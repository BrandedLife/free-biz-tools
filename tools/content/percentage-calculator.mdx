Use this calculator to work out what percentage one number is of another. Useful for analysing proportions, performance metrics, and business calculations.

## What this calculator does

The percentage calculator helps you find the percentage that a part represents of a whole.

It uses:

- part value  
- whole value  

This gives you the percentage result.

## How to use the percentage calculator

1. Enter the **part value**  
2. Enter the **whole value**  
3. The calculator will return the percentage  

Make sure the whole value is not zero.

## Percentage formula

**Percentage = (Part / Whole) x 100**

Where:

- **Part** = the smaller value or portion  
- **Whole** = the total value  
- **Percentage** = the proportion expressed as a percentage  

## Example calculation

If:

- Part = **25**  
- Whole = **200**  

Then:

- Percentage = **25 / 200 x 100**  
- Percentage = **12.5%**  

This means the part represents 12.5% of the whole.

## What is a percentage?

A percentage expresses a number as a portion of 100.

It is commonly used to compare values, measure changes, and understand proportions.

## Why percentages matter

Percentages help you:

- understand what share something represents  
- compare values easily  
- measure performance and growth  
- calculate discounts and margins  
- analyse conversion and marketing metrics  

## Percentage vs percentage change

These are different concepts.

- **Percentage** -> what portion of a whole something represents  
- **Percentage change** -> how much a value increases or decreases over time  

Use a percentage change calculator for growth or decline analysis.

Always ensure the inputs are correct.

## FAQs

### How do you calculate a percentage?

Percentage = (Part / Whole) x 100.

### What does percentage mean?

It shows how much one value represents out of a total of 100.

### Why is percentage useful?

It makes proportions easier to compare and understand.

### Can the whole be zero?

No. You cannot divide by zero, so the whole value must not be zero.

## Interpreting your result

Your percentage result should always be interpreted in context:

- confirm which value is the part and which is the whole
- compare the result against prior periods or benchmarks where relevant
- review it alongside the underlying absolute numbers
- avoid overinterpreting large percentages based on very small totals

A percentage is most useful when the underlying numbers are also understood.

## Data quality checklist

Before acting on this result, verify:

- the part and whole are correctly identified
- both values use the same unit of measure
- the whole is not zero
- the numbers come from the same time period or reporting basis

Small definition errors can completely change the meaning of a percentage.

Improvement is most reliable when the underlying business levers are identified first.

## FAQ extensions

### Can a percentage be above 100%?

Yes, depending on the context. Some ratios and growth comparisons can legitimately exceed 100%.

### Why does the same raw change look different in percentage terms?

Because percentage depends on the starting base. The same absolute change can look larger or smaller depending on the denominator.

### Should I report percentages without raw numbers?

Usually no. Raw values help people understand the scale behind the percentage.
