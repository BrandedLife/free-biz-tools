Use this calculator to estimate shipping profit or loss based on what you charge customers and what shipping actually costs.

## What this calculator does

The shipping profit calculator shows whether your shipping charge creates extra profit or causes a loss.

It is useful for:

- ecommerce pricing
- shipping strategy planning
- fulfillment analysis
- order profitability checks

## Shipping Profit Formula

**Shipping Profit = Shipping Charged − Actual Shipping Cost**

Where:

- **Shipping Charged** = amount billed to the customer
- **Actual Shipping Cost** = the real fulfillment or courier cost
- **Shipping Profit** = extra amount kept or lost on shipping

## Example calculation

If:

- Shipping charged = **10**
- Actual shipping cost = **7**

Then:

- Shipping profit = **10 − 7**
- Shipping profit = **3**

## What is shipping profit?

Shipping profit is the amount gained or lost after comparing what the customer paid for shipping with what shipping actually cost the business.

## Why shipping profit matters

Shipping profit matters because it helps businesses:

- avoid losing money on shipping
- build better delivery pricing
- understand per-order economics
- protect margins

## When to use this calculator

Use this calculator when you want to:

## Common mistakes

Common mistakes include:

- ignoring packaging costs
- excluding fulfillment labor
- using outdated courier rates
- assuming free shipping has no cost

## Shipping profit vs product profit

These are separate but related.

- **Shipping profit** measures gain or loss on shipping itself
- **Product profit** measures gain or loss on the item sold

## FAQs

### What is shipping profit?

Shipping profit is the difference between what a customer pays for shipping and what shipping actually costs.

### How do you calculate shipping profit?

Shipping Profit = Shipping Charged − Actual Shipping Cost.

### Why is shipping profit important?

It helps businesses understand whether shipping supports profit or creates hidden losses.

### Can shipping create a loss?

Yes. If actual shipping cost is higher than the amount charged, shipping creates a loss.
