Use this calculator to estimate net profit margin. Useful for understanding how much of your revenue is retained as profit after all expenses.

## What this calculator does

The net margin calculator helps you measure the percentage of revenue that remains as net profit after all costs are deducted.

It uses:

- net profit
- total revenue

This gives you **net margin (%)** — a key indicator of overall business profitability.

## How to use the net margin calculator

1. Enter your **net profit** (after all expenses, taxes, and interest)  
2. Enter your **total revenue** for the same period  
3. The calculator will return your net margin as a percentage  

Ensure both values are from the same timeframe.

## Net margin formula

**Net Margin (%) = (Net Profit ÷ Revenue) × 100**

Where:

- **Net Profit** = profit after all expenses  
- **Revenue** = total income from sales  
- **Net Margin** = percentage of revenue retained as profit  

## Example calculation

If:

- Net profit = **15000**  
- Revenue = **100000**  

Then:

- Net margin = **15000 ÷ 100000 × 100**  
- Net margin = **15%**  

This means the business keeps 15% of its revenue as profit.

## What is net margin?

Net margin is a profitability ratio that shows how much of your total revenue remains as profit after all expenses are deducted.

It reflects the **true bottom-line performance** of a business.

## How net margin affects business performance

Net margin indicates overall efficiency:

- higher net margin → more profitable business  
- lower net margin → tighter margins and higher cost pressure  
- negative margin → business is operating at a loss  

It is one of the most important financial health indicators.

## Why net margin matters

Tracking net margin helps you:

- understand true profitability  
- compare performance over time  
- benchmark against competitors  
- evaluate cost control and pricing strategy  
- support investor and lender reporting  

It provides a clear view of how efficiently your business operates.

## Net margin vs gross margin vs operating margin

These are related but measure different levels of profitability:

- **Gross margin** → profit after cost of goods sold (COGS)  
- **Operating margin** → profit from core operations  
- **Net margin** → final profit after all expenses  

Net margin is the most comprehensive measure.

## How to improve net margin

Businesses can improve net margin by:

- increasing prices or improving pricing strategy  
- reducing operating costs  
- improving supplier terms  
- optimising marketing spend  
- increasing efficiency and productivity  

Small improvements in margin can significantly impact profitability.

## Common mistakes when calculating net margin

Common mistakes include:

- using operating profit instead of net profit  
- excluding taxes, interest, or one-time costs  
- comparing different time periods  
- focusing only on margin without considering cash flow  
- using inconsistent revenue definitions  

Always use accurate and complete financial data.

### Is a higher net margin better?
