Use this calculator to measure the margin you retain after deducting cost of goods and channel fees from your selling price. Essential for ecommerce sellers, wholesalers, and multi-channel businesses evaluating the true profitability of each sales channel.

## What this calculator does

The channel margin calculator helps you measure how much margin remains from each sale after accounting for the cost of the product and any fees charged by the sales channel.

It uses:

- selling price
- cost per unit
- channel fee percentage

This gives you channel margin - the profit percentage retained after product cost and platform or distribution fees are deducted from the sale price.

## How to use the channel margin calculator

1. Enter your **selling price** - the price charged to the customer on the channel
2. Enter your **cost per unit** - the total direct cost of the product, including manufacturing, sourcing, and landed costs
3. Enter your **channel fee percentage** - the fee charged by the sales channel as a percentage of the selling price, such as Amazon's referral fee, a retailer's margin requirement, or a distributor's markup
4. The calculator instantly shows your channel margin amount and percentage

Different channels have very different fee structures. Always use the channel-specific fee rate for accurate results.

## Channel Margin Formula

**Channel Fee = Selling Price x Channel Fee %**

**Channel Margin = Selling Price - Cost Per Unit - Channel Fee**

**Channel Margin % = (Channel Margin / Selling Price) x 100**

Where:

- **Selling Price** = price charged to the customer on the channel
- **Cost Per Unit** = direct cost of the product
- **Channel Fee %** = fee charged by the sales channel as a percentage of selling price
- **Channel Margin** = profit retained after product cost and channel fees

## Example calculation

If:

- Selling price = **100**
- Cost per unit = **35**
- Channel fee = **15%**

Then:

- Channel fee amount = **100 x 0.15 = 15**
- Channel margin = **100 - 35 - 15 = 50**
- Channel margin % = **(50 / 100) x 100 = 50%**

After paying for the product and the channel fee, 50 remains - a channel margin of 50%.

## What is channel margin?

Channel margin is the profit percentage a seller retains after deducting the cost of goods and the fees or margin requirements of the sales channel through which a product is sold.

Different sales channels have very different cost structures. Selling directly through your own website typically has lower fees but requires you to drive your own traffic. Selling through marketplaces like Amazon or retailers like a wholesale partner gives you access to existing audiences but comes with significant margin requirements.

Understanding channel margin for each route to market helps you make informed decisions about which channels to prioritise, how to price products across channels, and whether a channel is worth selling through at all.

## Typical channel fee rates by channel type

Fee structures vary significantly across channels:

- **Amazon** - referral fees typically 8% to 17% depending on category, plus FBA fulfilment fees if applicable
- **Shopify direct** - payment processing typically 1.5% to 2.9% plus a small fixed fee
- **Etsy** - transaction fee of 6.5% plus listing and payment processing fees
- **Wholesale / retail distribution** - retailers typically require 40% to 60% gross margin, meaning the wholesale price is 40% to 60% below the retail selling price
- **Distributors** - typically take 15% to 30% of the wholesale price on top of the retailer's margin requirement
- **TikTok Shop** - commission fees typically 2% to 8% depending on category

Always verify current rates directly with each platform before making pricing decisions.

## Why channel margin matters for multi-channel businesses

Tracking channel margin helps you:

- compare the true profitability of each sales channel side by side
- identify which channels are worth growing and which are eroding margin
- set channel-appropriate pricing that preserves margin across all routes to market
- evaluate new channel opportunities before committing to them
- avoid the common mistake of selling at a loss on a channel because fees were not fully accounted for

## How to protect channel margin

Three practical approaches:

- **Price for each channel separately** - the same product may need different prices on different channels to deliver the same margin after fees
- **Negotiate channel fees** - high-volume sellers on some platforms can negotiate lower fee rates
- **Reduce cost per unit** - improving sourcing, manufacturing efficiency, or landed costs directly improves margin on every channel

## Common mistakes when calculating channel margin

Common mistakes include:

- using only the referral or commission fee and ignoring fulfilment, payment processing, or listing fees that also reduce margin
- comparing channel margins across different selling prices without adjusting for the full fee structure
- using landed cost per unit that does not include all import duties, freight, and handling costs
- assuming the same price can be used across all channels without adjusting for different fee structures

## Channel margin vs profit margin

These two metrics measure profitability at different levels.

- **Channel margin** measures the profit retained from a sale after product cost and channel-specific fees - it tells you whether a specific channel is worth using
- **Profit margin** measures the overall profitability of the business or product after all costs including overheads

A product can have a healthy channel margin but poor overall profit margin if operating costs, marketing spend, or other overheads are high. Use the [Profit Margin Calculator](/tools/profit-margin-calculator) to measure overall margin after all costs.

## FAQs

### What is channel margin?

Channel margin is the profit percentage retained from a sale after deducting the cost of goods and the fees or margin requirements of the sales channel.

### How do you calculate channel margin?

Channel Margin = Selling Price - Cost Per Unit - Channel Fee. Channel Margin % = (Channel Margin / Selling Price) x 100.

### What is a good channel margin?

It depends on the business model and channel. Direct-to-consumer channels typically offer higher margins of 40% to 70% or more. Wholesale and marketplace channels typically offer lower margins of 15% to 40% after fees and cost of goods. The key is whether margin is sufficient to cover overheads and generate profit.

### How do channel fees affect pricing strategy?

Channel fees must be factored into pricing from the start. A product priced for direct-to-consumer sales will often be unprofitable if listed on a marketplace at the same price without adjusting for the additional fee burden.

### Should I use the same price across all channels?

Not necessarily. Many sellers use channel-specific pricing to maintain consistent margin across different fee structures. Some platforms have policies around price parity - check the terms of each channel before setting differentiated prices.

### How do wholesale margins work?

When selling wholesale to retailers, the retailer applies their own margin on top of the wholesale price. The wholesale margin is the margin between your cost and the wholesale price you charge. The retailer then marks up from wholesale to retail. Use the [Wholesale Price Calculator](/tools/wholesale-price-calculator) to set a wholesale price that preserves your margin.

### Can channel margin be negative?

Yes. If the cost of goods plus channel fees exceeds the selling price, channel margin is negative - meaning you lose money on every sale. This is sometimes acceptable during a promotional period but is unsustainable as a long-term strategy.

### How often should I review channel margin?

Review channel margins whenever fees change, cost of goods change, or you are considering adding or removing a channel. Annual review is a minimum - quarterly is better for active multi-channel sellers.

## Interpreting your result

Your channel margin result should always be interpreted in context:

- compare it against your historical baseline
- review it alongside the main commercial or operational drivers behind the metric
- compare it across products, channels, periods, or segments where relevant
- avoid interpreting the result in isolation without checking the underlying input values

A single period can be noisy, so trend direction over several periods is usually more useful than one standalone result.

## Data quality checklist

Before acting on this result, verify:

- the inputs use the same time period and reporting basis
- one-off anomalies are identified separately from steady-state performance
- discounts, refunds, taxes, or fees are handled consistently where relevant
- the underlying values are complete enough to support a meaningful conclusion

Small input inconsistencies can materially change the result.

## How to improve this metric

Practical ways to improve this metric depend on the underlying business model, but often include:

- identify the main driver behind the result before making changes
- test one variable at a time so the impact is easier to measure
- compare performance by segment rather than only at an overall level
- review the metric regularly so changes can be caught early

Improvement is most reliable when measurement definitions remain stable over time.

## Benchmarks and target setting

A good target depends on your industry, business model, and stage of growth.

When setting targets:

- compare against your own historical trend before relying on outside benchmarks
- define both minimum acceptable and aspirational target ranges
- review targets whenever pricing, cost, demand, or channel mix changes materially
- pair benchmark review with the underlying commercial context, not just the final number

Your own historical performance is usually the most practical benchmark.

## Reporting cadence and decision workflow

For most teams, a simple cadence works best:

- **Weekly:** monitor the metric when trading conditions or campaign activity change quickly
- **Monthly:** compare the result against target and prior periods
- **Quarterly:** reassess assumptions, targets, and the main drivers behind the metric

A practical workflow is to calculate the metric, identify the primary driver of change, test one improvement, and then review the next comparable period before scaling.
